Technology

Digital Literacy and STEM

Technology at OTA is a tool for deeper learning, not a replacement for it. Our students use Chromebooks, coding platforms, and STEM challenges to extend what they learn in every subject.

Technology as a Learning Tool

Old Town Academy provides 1:1 Chromebooks for students, giving every learner access to research tools, collaborative documents, educational software, and creative platforms throughout the school day. But technology at OTA is always in service of learning, never a distraction from it. Screen time is purposeful. Every digital tool is chosen because it helps students do something they could not do (or could not do as well) without it.

Students use technology to conduct research for PBL projects, analyze data in science and math, create multimedia presentations, collaborate on shared documents, and communicate their learning to authentic audiences. Teachers integrate digital tools into their instruction deliberately, ensuring that technology enhances rather than replaces direct instruction, group discussion, and hands-on activities.

Digital Citizenship

Access to technology comes with responsibility, and OTA takes digital citizenship seriously. Beginning in the elementary grades, students learn about online safety, privacy, respectful communication, and the ethical use of information. They learn to evaluate sources critically, understand how algorithms shape what they see, and think carefully about the digital footprint they leave behind.

These lessons are not confined to a single unit. They are reinforced throughout the year as students use technology across subjects. When a student cites a source in a research project, that is digital citizenship in action. When a class discusses respectful commenting on a shared document, that is digital citizenship in practice.

Coding and Computational Thinking

OTA introduces coding and computational thinking as part of the broader STEM curriculum. Younger students begin with block-based coding platforms that develop logical thinking, sequencing, and problem-solving skills. As students progress into upper elementary and middle school, they work with more advanced tools including text-based programming and robotics coding through the Osprey Robotics program.

The goal is not to produce professional programmers (though some students may go on to that path). The goal is to develop computational thinking: the ability to break complex problems into smaller parts, identify patterns, design solutions, and test and refine those solutions through iteration. These skills are valuable in every subject and every career.

STEM Challenges

Hands-on STEM challenges are a regular part of the OTA experience, often integrated with project-based learning units. Students might design a structure to withstand simulated earthquake forces, build a solar oven and measure its efficiency, or prototype a water filtration system. These challenges develop engineering thinking, measurement skills, and collaborative problem-solving in a context that feels exciting and relevant.

Connecting to the Broader Curriculum

Digital literacy and STEM at OTA do not exist in isolation. They connect to Core Knowledge content (studying the history of technological innovation, for example), to character education (practicing integrity in how we use information), and to the arts (creating digital compositions and visual media). This integrated approach ensures that technology is not a separate silo but a thread running through the entire educational experience.
