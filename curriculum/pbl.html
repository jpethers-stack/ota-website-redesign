Hands-On Learning

Project-Based Learning

When students tackle real problems, present to real audiences, and connect classroom content to the world around them, learning sticks. That is the promise of PBL at Old Town Academy.

Learning by Doing

Project-based learning at OTA is not a separate subject. It is the bridge that connects what students learn in Core Knowledge classrooms to the world outside the school walls. When second graders study the water cycle, they do not stop at a textbook diagram. They collect rainfall data, build filtration models, and present their findings to families. When sixth graders study ecology, they investigate real San Diego ecosystems, from tidepools in Point Loma to the native plant communities along the San Diego River.

This approach ensures that academic knowledge is not abstract. Students see the relevance of what they learn because they use it to solve genuine problems, answer authentic questions, and create work that matters to an audience beyond their teacher.

Cross-Curricular Connections

Every PBL unit at OTA is designed to span multiple subjects. A project about local history might integrate reading (primary source analysis), writing (research essays), math (data collection and graphing), science (environmental change), and art (visual presentation). This cross-curricular structure reinforces the Core Knowledge principle that knowledge is interconnected, and it gives students practice in the kind of complex, multi-faceted thinking they will need in high school and beyond.

Student Voice and Presentation

A hallmark of PBL at OTA is the student presentation. At the end of each project cycle, students share their work with an audience: parents, community members, younger students, or visiting experts. These presentations build public speaking skills, confidence, and accountability. When students know their work will be seen by someone beyond their teacher, the quality of that work rises.

Presentations take many forms. They might be formal slide decks, museum-style exhibition boards, short films, live demonstrations, or panel discussions. The format depends on the project, but the expectation is consistent: communicate clearly, support your claims with evidence, and respond thoughtfully to questions.

Rooted in San Diego

OTA's location in the heart of Old Town, San Diego gives our PBL program a rich local context. Students have studied the history of the Old Town State Historic Park, measured water quality in local waterways, surveyed native plant species on neighborhood walks, and partnered with community organizations on service learning projects. This sense of place makes learning personal and connects students to the community that surrounds them.

PBL and STEM

Many of our PBL units incorporate STEM challenges that deepen the engineering and scientific thinking at the heart of each project. Students might design and build a bridge to meet load-bearing specifications, program a robot to navigate an obstacle course that mirrors a real-world scenario, or prototype a solution to a campus sustainability problem. These challenges connect naturally to our Digital Literacy and STEM program and our Robotics Program, creating a seamless learning experience across the school day.
